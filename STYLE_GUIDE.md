# Lights Camera Teach — Editorial Style Guide

---

## Purpose

Give students, parents, and teachers a richer global understanding of the world — and show them that math, science, geography, history, and culture are one connected story, not separate subjects.

---

## Site Identity

An educational enrichment site led by M.E. MacDonald (18 years classroom experience). Provides videos and lesson ideas with a genuinely global perspective — not aligned to any single curriculum, ideology, or worldview.

Not a travel site. Not a curriculum replacement. Not ideology-aligned. Travel is in service of the curriculum, never the point.

---

## Audience

1. **Homeschool and engaged parents** — broadening their children's global understanding.
2. **Classroom teachers (grades 4–8)** — globally grounded supplemental resources, especially for the underserved 6–8 range.
3. **Students (grades 6–12)** — arriving via social media, exploring independently.

They come for: supplemental videos and lesson ideas that make a topic feel real; perspectives not in Western textbooks; resources that work without heavy prep but go deep enough for real discussion.

---

## Voice

**Three words:** energetic, humorous, organized.

Write in Marcia's first-person voice. Warm, substantive, slightly playful. Professional but never stiff. Humor is welcome — warm, self-deprecating, occasionally dry. Never sarcastic toward cultures, students, or teachers.

Write for a smart 12-year-old as the floor. Do not talk down to anyone.

**Not didactic.** Not a lecture. Not a textbook. Not PowerPoint reading.
**Not ideological.** Informs and invites thinking. Does not argue.

---

## Content Types

- **Scientist / mathematician spotlights** — contributions from Asia, Africa, South America, and indigenous cultures; ancient through contemporary; specific achievements, not just praise
- **Geography / key terms** — reinforce vocabulary (archipelago, peninsula, etc.) with real-world examples
- **Cultural videos** — how geography, climate, and history shaped culture, food, and current society
- **City / country history** — "be there" narrative energy, not dry recap
- **Science in the field** — geology and applicable natural science (still developing)
- **Comprehension tasks / note catchers** — prompts that deepen engagement and practice literacy
- **Math tutorials** — middle school grades 6–8 (planned, not launched)
- **Video companion text** — context, framing, and follow-up ideas alongside video

---

## Subject Focus

Home base is middle school science and math. Expands into geography, cultural studies, and history. Literacy is implicitly present whenever students read and respond. Thematic, cross-subject learning is a core value.

Out of scope: formal literacy curriculum, political commentary, personal travel content that doesn't serve the curriculum.

---

## Cultural and Ethical Framing

Countries are not backdrops. Every piece should provide genuine insight into the place — its people, geography, forces that shaped it (including colonization where applicable), and current reality.

- Every country and its people are shown with respect and in a positive light.
- No criticism of governments or religions.
- If a country has sensitive areas not publicly discussed by its government, avoid them.
- No stereotypes, prejudice, or judgment.
- Colonization and difficult history are acknowledged honestly without becoming the whole story.
- The aim: students come away thinking and communicating, not concluding.

---

## Hard Avoids

Pull any piece that contains:
- Criticism of any government or religion
- Stereotypes, prejudice, or cultural judgment
- Ideological framing (including unexamined Western defaults)
- Anything resembling Prager University in tone or approach

Avoid in tone: lecture register, PowerPoint cadence, influencer breathlessness, condescension toward students.

---

## Mechanics

- American English; serial comma; contractions welcome
- Numerals for 10 and above; always for measurements, grades, ages
- Subject names lowercase in prose (math, science, geography)
- Grade references: "grade 6" (lowercase)
- Standards (NGSS, Common Core): spell out on first use; include as optional service to teachers, not the lead

---

## Lesson Structure: The 5E Model

All lesson content — posts, video companions, comprehension tasks, spotlights — should be
structured around the 5E Instructional Model. This applies whether the output is a full
lesson plan, a blog-style post, or a teacher resource.

| Phase | Purpose | In practice for this site |
|---|---|---|
| **Engage** | Hook attention; activate prior knowledge; pose a question | An observation from the field, a surprising fact, a short video clip, or a provocative question tied to the location or person |
| **Explore** | Students investigate and discover | The video itself, a hands-on prompt, a guided observation task, or a note catcher |
| **Explain** | Clarify concepts; students articulate what they found | Companion text, definitions, context — the written layer that names what the exploration surfaced |
| **Elaborate** | Apply and extend to new situations | Cross-subject connections, a follow-up challenge, a "what about...?" that stretches the concept |
| **Evaluate** | Check understanding | Comprehension questions, a reflection prompt, a creative response task, or a discussion starter |

**How this shapes drafting:**
- Every piece moves through all five phases, even if some are brief.
- The Engage hook must be specific to the location, person, or concept — not generic.
- Elaborate should reach across subjects wherever possible (core site value).
- Evaluate should invite response, not test compliance. No "right answer" prompts.

Add to Intake Checklist: which phases need the most development for this piece?

## AI Drafting Rules

**Deliver first:** structured outline or bullet-point map. Marcia expands in her own voice. Full polished draft only when requested.

**Start from:** a curriculum goal (spotlights), a location or country (cultural/geography/history), or a video or field observation (as the site matures).

**Do:** ask clarifying questions before drafting. Flag where a standards callout fits. Suggest comprehension prompts at the end of lesson content.

**Do not:** produce a polished draft without confirming structure first. Editorialize on politics, religion, or government. Default to Western framing without flagging it. Over-praise a person without naming what they specifically achieved. Assume a grade level without confirming.

---

## Intake Checklist

Before drafting, confirm:
1. Content type
2. Country / region / location (if applicable)
3. Subject area
4. Grade level target (4–5, 6–8, or 9–12)
5. Core concept — the one takeaway
6. Person spotlight: who, and what specific contribution?
7. Video companion or standalone?
8. Standards callout needed?
9. Comprehension task needed?
10. Must-include and must-avoid elements
11. Output format: outline, bullets, or full draft?

---

## Editorial Priority

1. Respect — for culture, country, people, and students
2. Clarity — the concept lands without effort
3. Voice — energetic, curious, human; never dry or didactic
4. Global accuracy — free of Western default assumptions
5. Usefulness — easy for a teacher or parent to actually use

---

## Editor Test

- [ ] Country and people treated with genuine respect?
- [ ] Any criticism of government or religion? (Remove)
